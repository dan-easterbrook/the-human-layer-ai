From Usage to Adoption

7–8 min read • Adoption

Organisations often mistake activity for progress when it comes to data and AI.

Tools are launched. Dashboards are refreshed. Pilots are announced.

Momentum appears visible.

Yet eighteen months later, a new leader arrives and the cycle begins again.

The issue is rarely capability. It is confusion between usage and adoption.

Usage is the act of turning something on.
Adoption is the act of changing how work is done.

The first can happen in a week.
The second requires sponsorship, patience and organisational design.

Until that distinction is understood, leaders burn out and progress resets.

The tenure pattern

Many data leaders inherit fragmented systems, inconsistent definitions and a board expecting visible transformation within two quarters.

Visible artefacts can be delivered quickly.

Foundations cannot.

Governance, metric alignment, lineage, access models and cultural norms move at a different speed.

When transformation does not arrive as fast as hoped, organisations often change the individual rather than the horizon.

Another restart follows.

Compounding never occurs.

Enterprise adoption is a multi-year arc with quarterly proof points, not quarterly reinvention.

Role design shapes outcomes

Senior hiring briefs frequently contain contradictions.

Strategic leader.
Deeply hands on.
Able to operate at board level and jump into tickets.

This is rarely clarity. It is unresolved organisational tension.

Sponsorship is behaviour, not agreement

Data quality and AI adoption do not sit inside the data team.

True sponsorship shows up in rituals.

Cross-functional forums where definitions are approved.
Metrics are retired.
Blockers are removed.
Infrastructure is funded.

Quick wins and silent costs

Under pressure, teams default to speed.

A new dashboard.
A rapid automation.
Another platform purchase.

Adoption includes subtraction.

AI introduces a new layer of ambiguity

Interest in AI has accelerated experimentation.

Guardrails are more durable than bans.

Beyond dashboards

Stakeholders often want decisions, not charts.

Consumption matters more than creation.

Capacity creation requires intent

Productivity gains create surplus capacity.

Adoption is not only technical change. It is workforce design.

Patterns across the first 90 days

Days 0–30
Align ambition. Select one owned workflow. Publish guardrails. Define metrics.

Days 31–60
Design the workflow. Pilot with real users. Iterate visibly.

Days 61–90
Ship to production. Measure impact. Retire what was replaced.

What stable adoption looks like

Executives attend adoption forums.
Certified metrics are trusted.
Legacy processes are retired.

None of this is theatrical.

All of it compounds.

The largest unlock is rarely a model choice.

It is the decision to build stewardship first.
